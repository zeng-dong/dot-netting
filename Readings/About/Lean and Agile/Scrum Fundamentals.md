### I am watch a training video on Scrum Fundamentals and the speaker says "Scrum defines a set of values and declares these values as part of the framework. This means that if you aren't exhibiting the behavior that goes along with these values, you aren't using Scrum.". I totally accept it. But I would like to have some detailed reasoning for it. Could you elaborate on it?

The speaker is making a strong philosophical claim about Scrum that goes deeper than just process mechanics. Let me walk through the reasoning:

**Scrum as a coherent system**

Scrum isn't just a collection of ceremonies and artifacts you can cherry-pick from. It's designed as an integrated framework where the values (Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, Courage) provide the foundation that makes the practices actually work. The ceremonies exist _because_ of the values, not independently of them.

**Why values aren't optional**

Consider what happens without each value:

Without _Openness_, Sprint Reviews become performances rather than honest inspections. The team hides problems, stakeholders don't share real feedback, and the empirical process breaks down completely.

Without _Courage_, teams won't surface impediments, challenge unrealistic commitments, or push back on scope creep. The Daily Scrum becomes a status report to avoid accountability rather than a planning session.

Without _Respect_, cross-functional collaboration fails. Developers dismiss QA concerns, Product Owners override the team's capacity judgments, and the self-organizing team structure collapses into dysfunction.

**The mechanical compliance trap**

You could theoretically hold all the ceremonies, maintain a backlog, and do two-week sprints while everyone operates with secrecy, fear, and disrespect. You'd have the _form_ of Scrum but none of its benefits. The transparency that enables inspection and adaptation would be an illusion. This is often called "Scrumbut" or "Zombie Scrum."

**Empiricism requires trust**

Scrum's empirical foundation (inspect and adapt) only works when people honestly share what they see. That honesty requires the psychological safety that comes from the values. Without the values, you can't get real transparency, which means you can't truly inspect, which means you can't meaningfully adapt.

So the speaker's point is: if you're not living the values, you may be following a process that _looks_ like Scrum, but you've gutted the very thing that makes Scrum effective. You're essentially performing a ritual without understanding its purpose.

### Thanks. Can you elaborate on "focus", what it means, and what effects a team could cause when the team fails to focus.

**What "Focus" means in Scrum**

In the Scrum context, Focus means the team concentrates their effort on the work of the Sprint and the goals of the Scrum Team. It's about limiting work in progress, maintaining a clear Sprint Goal, and resisting the constant pull of distractions and competing priorities.

Focus operates at multiple levels:

_Sprint level_ - The team commits to a Sprint Goal and the selected backlog items, and everyone's energy goes toward achieving that goal rather than scattered across dozens of initiatives.

_Daily level_ - Team members work on completing items before starting new ones, moving work across the board rather than having everything "in progress" simultaneously.

_Strategic level_ - The Product Owner focuses the backlog on a coherent product vision rather than trying to be everything to everyone.

**What happens when teams fail to focus**

Without focus, several predictable dysfunctions emerge:

**Work fragmentation** - Team members juggle five tasks simultaneously, making genuine progress on none. Context-switching overhead destroys productivity. Nothing reaches "Done" until the last days of the Sprint, eliminating any chance for early feedback or course correction.

**Loss of the Sprint Goal's power** - If the Sprint Goal is vague or ignored, the Sprint becomes just a random two-week time box. The team can't make intelligent trade-offs because they don't know what they're optimizing for. When obstacles arise, they can't determine what to sacrifice and what to protect.

**Inability to say no** - Without focus, teams accept every interrupt, every "quick favor," every urgent request from stakeholders. The Sprint backlog becomes meaningless as a commitment. Planning becomes theater because everyone knows the plan will be abandoned by Wednesday.

**Quality deterioration** - Scattered attention means cut corners. Testing gets deferred, technical debt accumulates, and the Definition of Done erodes into "mostly works on my machine."

**Reduced transparency** - When everyone is working on everything, status becomes murky. The Sprint Burndown chart flatlines until suddenly everything is marked done at once. Impediments hide in the noise because no one is looking at any single thing long enough to notice problems.

**Team demoralization** - There's something psychologically defeating about never finishing anything. Teams that lack focus experience constant low-grade stress, never get the satisfaction of completion, and eventually become cynical about the process.

**Example scenario**

Imagine a team mid-Sprint: Sarah is 80% done with the authentication feature but gets pulled into an "emergency" customer support issue. John starts working on a new story before finishing his current one because he's "waiting on QA." The Product Owner adds a "small" feature request mid-Sprint. Nobody wants to seem uncooperative, so everyone says yes to everything.

By Sprint end, they have eight items 70% complete and nothing shippable. They have no working software to demonstrate, can't get meaningful feedback, and carry everything forward to the next Sprint. They've been busy but accomplished nothing of value.

**Focus enables flow**

The paradox is that by narrowing focus, teams actually deliver _more_. Finishing three items completely is more valuable than having ten items in progress. A clear Sprint Goal lets the team move as a unit rather than as individuals. Focus creates the conditions for genuine collaboration, quality work, and predictable delivery.

# The Role of Lean Thinking

Lean thinking is foundational to the theory behind Scrum

what does it mean to be a Lean thinker? Scrum's been heavily influenced by Lean, which has been successfully applied to software development for the last 20 years or so. 
Lean thinking is the philosophy of identifying and removing waste from a system. 
Taking Lean thinking to heart, the automobile industry in Japan began building on Lean in the 1950s, which turned around their companies from producing poorly made and defect‑prone vehicles to becoming some of the most reliable on the world market. Lean manufacturing is a business model that seeks to deliver high‑quality products or services as efficiently as possible, and efficiency is a huge component of Lean thinking. 
It's grounded, as we said, in eliminating waste, but also in expanding capacity to do work by shortening cycle times and understanding what the customer needs and what is important to them. 
The Toyota Way is a set of principles driven by Lean thinking defined by Toyota. Toyota first summed up its philosophy, values, and manufacturing ideals in 2001, and this is what they called the Toyota Way. 

It consists of principles in two key areas, continuous improvement and respect for people. 

The Toyota Way has been designated a framework intended to give the instruments to individuals to constantly improve their work, and you can learn more about the Toyota Way in this book by Jeffrey K. Liker. NUMMI stands for New United Motor Manufacturing, Incorporated. This was a partnership between Toyota and General Motors. It was a facility that opened in 1984. Toyota was trying to teach the Toyota Way to GM, and it didn't go so well. In fact, it was a total failure because of GM's inability to adapt its culture. The podcast that I'm showing here on the screen, This American Life, did an episode on NUMMI, the URL for which you can see on the screen. It's an in‑depth examination of this amazing story. I'm including this simply because I loved the episode, and I learned a lot from it. I hope you will too. 

Lean's five principles. 

applying the five Lean principles is a continuous process without end
First, Lean says that value is anything the customer is willing to pay for. It is important to discover the actual needs of the customer, not just what they want. And often customers may not know what they need or are unable to articulate it. 
There is a step that we perform next, and that is mapping the value stream. The goal is to use the customer's value as a reference point and identify all the activities that we, as a product development team, need to go through to deliver this value. With something called a value stream map, we document every step of a value delivery pipeline. It becomes much easier to identify the waste in the system and eliminate it. Next, we look at creating flow. After eliminating waste from the system, we ensure that the flow of the remaining steps run smoothly without interruption or delays. This is the act of creating flow. Next, we establish pull. The goal of a pull‑based system is to limit inventory and work in process, enabling a smooth flow of work. Now, a pull‑based system enables just‑in‑time delivery where products are created at the time they are needed and in just the right quantities that are needed. The fifth step of pursuing perfection may be the most important customer needs. Here the company becomes a learning organization and finds ways to get a little bit better each and every day. The Kanban method is a means to design, manage, and improve flow systems
