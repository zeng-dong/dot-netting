# Design Thinking Fundamentals

## ### Define: Framing the Problem

The define stage of design thinking is the second stage. Simply put, the define stage is where you establish what problem you're trying to solve. After immersing ourselves with users and our customers in the empathize stage where we've collected a wealth of data and insights, it's now important to crystallize these into a cogent and actionable problem statement. A problem statement isn't just about identifying the issue. It's also about framing it in a way that will foster innovative solutions. To do this effectively, you have to incorporate your observations from the empathize stage. Tim Brown, the CEO of IDEO, said in his book, Change by Design, that "the relationship between analysis and synthesis plays a critical role in creating options and making choices. Analysis helps us break down complex problems into manageable parts, while synthesis allows us to creatively assemble these parts into ideas. This synthesis happens as we define our problem statement, guiding the subsequent ideation phase. 

The problem statement should be human‑centered. It should emphasize real human experiences and needs, rather than technical jargon or product specs. 
It should be broad enough to invite creative exploration, yet narrow enough to remain manageable. 
For example, if an elderly user finds touchscreen interfaces challenging, a well‑framed problem statement might be, how might we design an interface that enhances usability for elderly users without straining their eyesight or dexterity? An effective problem definition often involves reframing. This means looking at a problem from a different angle and questioning what's really preventing the users from achieving their goals. This can uncover new pathways for solutions that were previously unconsidered. Collaboration is key in this stage. The more input you have from members of your team, the more perspectives it brings to the table, which helps you refine the problem statement. It ensures that it's grounded in deep collective understanding. By involving the entire team, we leverage a variety of insights, which may include using methods like empathy mapping, where observations about what users say, do, think, and feel help sharpen our focus. Once we've crafted a refined problem statement, it sets the direction for ideation. This stage isn't just about solving a problem; it's about solving the right problem, which makes design thinking so impactful. Your problem statement should use action verbs, like create, define, or adapt, helping to move the team forward to a solution. By articulating a point of view that combines knowledge of the user, their needs, and our insights, we create a foundation for "how might we" questions. These questions, broad, yet specific, guide our ideation sessions, sparking creativity, and ensuring that our solutions are both innovative and aligned with user needs. The clarity that we gain from this stage prepares us for the ideation stage, giving us the ability to explore potential solutions with enthusiasm and a clear focus.
